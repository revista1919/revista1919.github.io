01. Important Notice on Editorial Policies

Submitting a manuscript to the National Review of Sciences for Students implies full and unreserved acceptance of the entirety of our Editorial Policies. We urge you to read them carefully before preparing your contribution. Failure to comply with any of their provisions —regarding originality, ethics, authorship, conflicts of interest, or any other matter— may result in the rejection of the submission at any stage of the editorial process.

02. Document Format and Word Processors

2.1. Mandatory Format

All manuscripts must be submitted exclusively in Microsoft Word (.docx) format. This is the standard working format for the Journal's review, editing, and typesetting process. Submissions in other formats (PDF, LaTeX, RTF, TXT, Google Docs, etc.) will not be accepted.

2.2. Instructions for LaTeX Users

The Journal recognizes that LaTeX is a widely used tool in disciplines such as mathematics, physics, engineering, and computer science. However, to standardize the editorial workflow, the final manuscript must be delivered in .docx format. To this end, authors writing in LaTeX must convert their document before submission.

The use of conversion tools such as Pandoc, a free and open-source document converter, is recommended. Below is an example command to convert a .tex file to .docx:

pandoc archivo.tex --citeproc --csl=chicago-author-date.csl -o resultado.docx

The use of a CSL (Citation Style Language) style file and a BibTeX bibliography file is highly recommended to preserve the integrity of citations and references during conversion.

It is the author's sole responsibility to verify that the conversion has faithfully preserved the content, mathematical formulas, cross-references, and bibliographic citations. It is suggested to meticulously review the resulting .docx file before proceeding with the submission. The Journal is not responsible for errors or formatting losses resulting from a deficient conversion.

2.3. Font Format and Styles

The font type, size, line spacing, and margins of the document submitted by the author are not relevant for the initial evaluation, as the manuscript will be automatically standardized during the typesetting process. The essential point is that the content is clear and that the document structure is hierarchized through the correct use of headings (see Section 04).

03. Anonymization for Double-Blind Review

3.1. General Principle

The Journal operates a double-blind peer review system. To ensure its integrity, the submitted manuscript must not contain any information that could identify its authors, either directly or indirectly.

3.2. Elements That Must Be Removed or Anonymized

Before submitting the document, authors must ensure that the following identifying elements have been removed or replaced:

  • Author names and their institutional affiliations on the title page and anywhere else in the manuscript.
  • Email addresses, phone numbers, and postal addresses of the authors.
  • Acknowledgments that mention individuals, institutions, or projects that could reveal the authors' identity. This section must be removed from the manuscript for review and may be reinstated in the final accepted version.
  • Funding information that includes grant codes or references to specific projects that could identify the authors. It may be mentioned generically (e.g., "This work was supported by a national doctoral scholarship") if strictly necessary, but it is preferable to omit it.
  • Self-citations that could reveal identity. References to the authors' previous works should appear in the text as "(Author, year)" and in the reference list as "[Anonymized for review]." After acceptance, full citations will be restored.
  • File metadata: The .docx file must not contain author information in its properties (File > Info > Properties). It is recommended to use Word's "Inspect Document" tool to remove personal data before saving the final version for submission.
  • Names of specific institutions or laboratories in the Methods section, if these are unique and easily traceable to the authors. Use generic descriptions (e.g., "a university hospital in the metropolitan region").

04. Manuscript Structure and Use of Headings

4.1. The Importance of Headings

The structure of an academic manuscript must not be indicated by text in bold or with a larger font size applied manually. Authors are required to use the heading functions (Heading 1, Heading 2, Heading 3, etc.) of their word processor. This is of vital importance to the Journal for the following reasons:

  1. Automatic Styling: The Journal applies automatic formatting styles to all accepted manuscripts. A document that uses real headings can be formatted in seconds; a document with "fake headings" requires costly and error-prone manual formatting.
  2. Navigation and Evaluation: Headings automatically generate a navigation panel in the word processor, allowing editors and reviewers to move agilely through the document and understand its structure at a glance.
  3. Accessibility: Semantic headings are essential for assistive technologies (such as screen readers used by people with visual impairments) to navigate and understand the document hierarchy.

4.2. Correct Use of the Heading Hierarchy

The standard heading hierarchy and its application in a typical manuscript are detailed below. Please respect the order and logical nesting. Do not skip from one heading level to another (for example, from Title to Heading 3 without passing through Heading 2).

Level Style in Word / LaTeX Recommended Use
1 Heading 1 / \section{} Title of the main sections: "Introduction," "Methods," "Results," "Discussion," "Conclusion," "References."
2 Heading 2 / \subsection{} Subsections within a main section. For example, within "Methods": "Participants," "Instruments," "Procedure."
3 Heading 3 / \subsubsection{} More specific subdivisions. For example, within "Instruments": "Demographic Data Questionnaire," "Psychological Well-Being Scale."

Note on LaTeX: If you write in LaTeX, using \section, \subsection, and \subsubsection automatically generates the correct heading structure. When converting to .docx with Pandoc, these commands will be translated to the "Heading 1," "Heading 2," and "Heading 3" styles in Word, provided an appropriate reference file is used.

Warning: A manuscript that presents its sections only with bold text and without using the word processor's heading styles may be returned to the authors for correction before the review process begins.

4.3. Recommended Structure for an Original Research Article (IMRaD Format)

The following structure is recommended for empirical articles. Other manuscript types (essays, reviews) may adapt it to their nature, always maintaining a logical heading hierarchy.

  • Heading 1: Introduction (Presents the problem, research question, and objectives)
  • Heading 1: Methods
    • Heading 2: Design
    • Heading 2: Participants
    • Heading 2: Instruments
    • Heading 2: Procedure
    • Heading 2: Data Analysis
  • Heading 1: Results
  • Heading 1: Discussion
  • Heading 1: Conclusion
  • Heading 1: References

05. Recommended Length

5.1. Standard Range

The recommended length for all manuscript types is 1,000 to 10,000 words, including the body of the text, footnotes, acknowledgments (in the final version), and the reference list. This range is considered adequate for most research papers submitted by students and young researchers, allowing sufficient development without being excessive.

5.2. Manuscripts Exceeding the Recommended Length

The Journal is aware that certain works —such as extensive systematic reviews, studies with multiple experiments, or adapted theses— may naturally exceed the 10,000-word limit. Manuscripts of greater length will be accepted. However, such works will be subject to an additional review criterion by the editor in charge:

  • The editor will assess whether the length is fully justified by the nature and complexity of the study.
  • If the length is deemed excessive and does not add substantial value (for example, due to verbosity, repetition of information, or inclusion of tangential content), the editor may suggest a cut as a condition for continuing the review process or for final acceptance.
  • In extreme cases, if the manuscript exceeds twice the maximum recommended length (20,000 words) and is not considered a monumental contribution, the editor may suggest its division into two or more independent publications.

The final decision on the acceptance of a manuscript of atypical length rests with the editor, who will communicate it to the authors with supporting reasons.

06. Citation Style: Chicago 17th ed. (Author-Date)

6.1. General Rule

All manuscripts, without exception, must format their scholarly apparatus according to the Chicago Manual of Style, 17th edition, in its author-date variant. The correct and consistent use of this style is a requirement for manuscript acceptance.

6.2. In-Text Citations

Citations are inserted in the body of the text in parentheses, indicating the author's surname, the year of publication, and, when relevant, the page(s).

Examples:

"The theory of social constructivism has been widely debated in recent decades (González 2020, 45-48)."

"According to Martínez and López (2019), the results suggest a significant correlation..."

"For a more detailed analysis, see Rodríguez et al. (2022)." (For three or more authors, use "et al." from the first citation).

Personal communications (emails, conversations) are cited only in the text and are not included in the reference list: (Juan Pérez, personal communication, March 15, 2024).

6.3. Reference List

All references cited in the text must be listed at the end of the manuscript under the heading "References" (Heading 1), arranged alphabetically by the first author's surname. Detailed examples of the most frequent document types are provided below. For any other type not covered here, consult the official manual at Chicago Manual of Style Online or contact the editorial team.

Book (single author):

Binder, Amy J. 2022. The Channels of Student Activism: How the Left and Right Are Winning (and Losing) in Campus Politics Today. Chicago: University of Chicago Press.

Book (two or more authors):

Levitt, Steven D., and Stephen J. Dubner. 2005. Freakonomics: A Rogue Economist Explores the Hidden Side of Everything. New York: William Morrow.

Chapter in an edited book:

Doyle, Kathleen. 2023. "The Queen Mary Psalter." In The Book by Design: The Remarkable Story of the World's Greatest Invention, edited by P. J. M. Marks and Stephen Parkin, 60-73. Chicago: University of Chicago Press.

Journal article:

Dittmar, Emily L., and Douglas W. Schemske. 2023. "Temporal Variation in Selection Influences Microgeographic Local Adaptation." American Naturalist 202 (4): 471-85. https://doi.org/10.1086/725865.

Thesis or dissertation:

Blajer de la Garza, Yuna. 2019. "A House Is Not a Home: Citizenship and Belonging in Contemporary Democracies." PhD diss., University of Chicago. ProQuest (13865986).

Web page:

Google. 2023. "Privacy Policy." Privacy & Terms. Effective November 15. https://policies.google.com/privacy.

Newspaper or magazine article:

Blum, Dani. 2023. "Are Flax Seeds All That?" New York Times, December 13. https://www.nytimes.com/2023/12/13/well/eat/flax-seeds-benefits.html.

Important Notes on Reference Formatting:

  • The place of publication is no longer mandatory for books according to the 17th edition, but it is recommended to include it when relevant.
  • For articles with more than six authors, list the first three followed by "et al." in the full reference.
  • Whenever available, include the DOI (Digital Object Identifier) at the end of the reference for journal articles.
  • If a source has no identifiable publication date, use "n.d." (no date).

07. Keywords and Controlled Vocabularies

All manuscripts submitted as of Volume 2, Issue 2 must include a minimum of two (2) and a maximum of six (6) keywords, selected from the official controlled vocabularies. Consult the complete mapping of disciplines to controlled vocabularies in the Editorial Policies to find the corresponding thesaurus for your area and the consultation link.

Each keyword must be presented in the format Code: Term (e.g., D009369: Neoplasms for MeSH), in both Spanish and English. The submission form will guide you through this process.

08. Figures, Tables, and Graphic Material

Figures and tables must be inserted in the approximate place in the text where they are first mentioned, with their corresponding numbering and caption. Consult the Guidelines for Figures and Images in the Editorial Policies for technical, scientific integrity, and third-party permission requirements.

10. Submission Checklist

Before submitting your manuscript, verify that it meets all points on this list. A submission that does not meet these basic requirements will be returned for correction, delaying the editorial process.

  1. The manuscript is in .docx format. If LaTeX was used, it has been correctly converted and the result verified.
  2. The length is within the recommended range of 1,000 to 10,000 words. If longer, it has been considered whether the excess is justified.
  3. The document is completely anonymized: it contains no author names, affiliations, acknowledgments, or identifying metadata.
  4. The word processor's heading function (Heading 1, Heading 2, etc.) has been used to structure the manuscript. The structure has not been simulated with bold text.
  5. Citations and references strictly follow the Chicago 17th ed. (author-date) style. Each citation and its corresponding reference have been reviewed.
  6. Between 2 and 6 keywords with their controlled vocabulary code have been included, in both Spanish and English.
  7. Figures and tables meet the resolution, format, and caption requirements.
  8. The Journal's Editorial Policies have been read and are fully accepted.
